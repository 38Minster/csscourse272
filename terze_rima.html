Terze Rima

Column 1

The terze Rima is a particularly special scheme. It is hardly used in English, Italian, on the other hand, gives itself, so I am led to believe, especially to this form, In which Dante wrote The Inferno and it is thought that Dante may have invented the form. Strangely both the Lonfellow and the recent Clive James translations of this work have changed the Terze Rima of the poem for the more 'English' Quattrain, which James describes as more 'driving' and implies is more suitable for a poem as exciting as Inferno. like all the poetic forms in this section, the form does not specify a metre, all that is important is that : it is written in three-line stanzas (called tercets) the end of the middle line of each tercet rhymes with the first and last line of the next tercet. Here is an example:

Acquainted with the Night


I have been one acquainted with the night.
I have walked out in rain and back in rain.
I have outwalked the furthest city light.

I have looked down the saddest city lane.
I have passed by the watchman on his beat
And dropped my eyes, unwilling to explain.

I have stood still and stopped the sound of feet
When far away an interrupted cry
Came over houses from another street,

But not to call me back or say good-bye;
And further still at an unearthly height,
One luminary clock against the sky

Proclaimed the time was neither wrong nor right.
I have been one acquainted with the night.

Robert Frost
